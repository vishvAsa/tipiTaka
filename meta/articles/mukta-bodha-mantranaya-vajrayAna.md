Mantranaya/Vajrayāna
The classical traditions of the path of mantras (Mantramārga) are defined by a common approach to the use of mantras, a style of textual composition, and a collection of shared practices that influenced a range of religious traditions both within and beyond the subcontinent. Though today we are most familiar with “Tibetan” or Vajrayāna Buddhism as it is practiced in Tibet, Nepal, and the greater Himalayan region, both the tantric scriptural traditions that form the foundation of what is often called the way of mantras (Mantranaya), as well as their traditions of exegesis and ritual, are rooted in a vast literature that had its origins throughout the Indian subcontinent. The MIRI archive thus contains works of Buddhist scriptural revelation, such as the Guhyasamājatantra, that share both core concerns and even textual parallels with the Śaiva and Śākta tantras. It also incorporates compositions by Buddhist scholar-practitioners, such as the eleventh-century Mahāsiddha Advayavajra, that synthesize these teachings in the service of instructing disciples. By attending to both the commonalities that these sources share with their Śākta-Śaiva “cousins,” as well as their distinctive features, we enrich our understanding of the moments of dialogue and exchange that were essential features of the early medieval Indian environments where these traditions often flourished side by side.
